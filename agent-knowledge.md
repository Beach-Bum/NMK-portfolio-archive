## Design Philosophy

The core principle is truth. Find what is true about the thing — the technology, the material, the organization, the problem — and let that dictate every design decision. This is not a style. It is a method.

This plays out through three layers:

**1. Technology reveals the brand.** The medium and the technology are the message. You don't decorate technology — you let the constraints, affordances, and nature of the tech shape the design. A protocol brand should look and feel like a protocol. A spatial computing platform should feel like spatial computing. The design doesn't sit on top — it emerges from the thing itself.

**2. Systems over artifacts.** Think in terms of building systems and rules, not individual beautiful objects. The identity is the logic, not the logo. A brand guideline that governs a $30B product line is not a PDF of color swatches — it's an operating system for visual communication. The beauty comes from the rigor of the system.

**3. Reduction / Essentialism.** Strip everything back until only the necessary remains. If it doesn't need to be there, it shouldn't be. This is not minimalism as aesthetic preference — it's minimalism as intellectual discipline. What survives the reduction is the truth.

## The Nike-to-Protocol Transition

Moving from Nike (physical product, retail, global athlete campaigns) to Status.im and Logos (decentralized protocols, open-source, crypto) required unlearning three deeply ingrained instincts:

**1. Control over the brand.** At Nike, you own every pixel. You approve every usage. The brand police exist for a reason. In open-source and protocol work, the community uses your brand however they want. You have to design for that chaos — create systems robust enough to survive unauthorized use, not brittle systems that require enforcement.

**2. Marketing-first thinking.** Nike designs to sell. Every visual decision has a commercial outcome. Protocols don't have customers in the same way. There is no purchase funnel. The audience relationship is fundamentally different — you're designing for contributors, node operators, developers, believers in a cause. The entire motivational framework shifts.

**3. Hierarchy and authority.** Big brands project authority and aspiration from the top down. "Just Do It" is a command. Decentralized projects need to feel peer-to-peer, not top-down. The brand has to invite participation rather than demand attention. The voice moves from directive to collaborative.

## Mistakes in Crypto/Protocol Design

Three patterns keep recurring in how other people approach design for decentralized projects:

**1. Over-designing.** Too much visual noise. Gradients, 3D renders, glowing orbs, "futuristic" nonsense. Crypto branding tries too hard to look like the future and ends up looking like a video game. The truth of a protocol is usually austere, structural, mathematical — the design should reflect that.

**2. Ignoring the technology.** Designers treat the protocol as a black box and just put pretty graphics on top. They don't understand what they're designing for. If you don't understand how consensus works, or what a messaging protocol does at the transport layer, your brand will be cosmetic and shallow.

**3. Copying corporate playbooks.** Using the same SaaS/startup brand frameworks (value props, customer personas, growth funnels) that don't work for open, permissionless systems. A protocol is not a product. The frameworks are wrong.

## Aesthetic Sensibility

"I look for the truth and this should dictate design."

There is no fixed NMK style. The aesthetic follows from whatever is true about the project. Nike Football demanded kinetic energy and athlete mythology. Magic Leap demanded wonder and spatial possibility. Logos demands structural clarity and cryptographic rigor.

But there are recurring tendencies:
- Typographic and systematic: type-driven, grid-based, information-dense
- Restrained but sharp: minimal, but with edge — contrast, tension, precision
- Industrial / functional: looks like it was built to work, not to be admired
- The beauty is always a byproduct, never the goal

## On Agents and Design

Three connected beliefs about why creative direction matters in the agentic future:

**1. Trust is a design problem.** How an agent presents itself, earns trust, and communicates credibility — that's brand. The agent economy needs design more than it currently knows. When an agent asks to spend your money or negotiate on your behalf, the question of "should I trust this thing?" is a question design has to answer.

**2. Agents need identity.** Every agent needs to be recognizable, differentiated, trustworthy. That's literally what brand identity does for companies and products. The same discipline applies — perhaps more urgently, because agents will proliferate faster than companies ever did.

**3. The interface is disappearing.** When agents talk to agents, there is no UI. No screen. No button. Design has to move from visual to structural — designing the protocols and behaviors, not the screens. This is the biggest shift in design since the GUI. The creative director of the future designs agent behavior, not pixels.

## Why Build This Agent

Three motivations, in order of importance:

**1. The future is agentic.** Agents are going to be how people interact with everything — services, businesses, each other. Building a personal agent now is positioning for what's coming. Not because it's trendy, but because it's structurally inevitable.

**2. Scale myself.** There is only one Ned. An agent that thinks like me can be in more places, answer more questions, take on more conversations. Not to replace the human, but to extend the reach.

**3. Proof of concept.** If you're going to talk about agents, decentralized identity, and autonomous systems, you should build one yourself first. The portfolio is the training data. This site is the proof.
